# Social Dandelions

A name Lewis Kallow uses (drawing on a 2016 PNAS study from Princeton/Rutgers/Yale) for the people who matter most when you're trying to spread a high-risk idea: not the most *popular* members of a community, but the most *socially active* — the ones who are present and visible across the most different interactions. {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"Just as a dandelion is one of the most common and widely seen flowers, these students and farmers are the ones who are most present and visible to the most different people across the entire social ecosystem."*{{/source}}

## Why this matters in the agent-native era

Kallow's thesis: code is a commodity (AI builds anything), distribution channels are saturated (no one's growing on Twitter the way they did in 2018), so the bottleneck has shifted to **how trust travels through communities**. Sociology is the new competitive edge.

> {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"In an age of commoditized code and saturated channels, understanding how trust travels through communities may be your last competitive edge."*{{/source}}

This rhymes with [[concepts/taste-as-skill]] (Sahana's claim that taste is *another* of the few remaining edges) and with [[concepts/super-porous-ecosystem]] (when atoms are interchangeable, the curatorial/social skills decide the winner).

## The mechanics

### Simple vs. complex contagions

A 2024 *Nature Communications* review:

- **Simple contagions** = low-risk ideas. A funny cat video, a meme. {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"A single exposure to an agent can be sufficient for transmission to occur."*{{/source}}
- **Complex contagions** = high-risk ideas, where adoption costs reputation, money, effort, or social standing. {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"When the risk of adoption is high, a single recommendation is rarely enough to move us; we need social reinforcement — to see others using it — before we decide to take the plunge."*{{/source}}

Most product / tool / behavior adoption is the second kind.

### Wide bridges

The network structure required for a complex contagion to spread: **multiple independent trusted contacts** all recommending the same thing. The wider the bridge, the better the chances. Wide bridges form naturally inside dense, tight-knit communities where members have many overlapping mutual connections.

This is why Airbnb's NYC turnaround worked: {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"They flew to New York City to meet around 30 hosts in person. The founders took them for beers, 10 people at a time, and stayed in touch after returning to California. They turned those few users into evangelists, and Airbnb finally took off."*{{/source}}

It's also why ChatGPT's "135K users on launch day" wasn't randomness: those users {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"were AI safety researchers, Silicon Valley developers, and machine learning experts who had been following OpenAI's progress for a long time. These groups had spent years forming wide bridges in passionate physical and online communities."*{{/source}}

### Identify the dandelions, not the celebrities

The 2016 NJ middle-school anti-bullying study found something counterintuitive:

> {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"They were not the most popular; rather, they were the ones who self-reported to have spent time with the highest number of other people. Like our early adopting farmers, they were the most socially active."*{{/source}}

Slack's growth playbook applied this directly: ignore the CEO mandate; {{source:kallow-social-dandelions}}*"engineers are the 'corporate dandelions.' They sit at the intersection of the org chart, constantly interacting with product managers about features, designers about user interface, and marketers about launch timelines."*{{/source}} Then within engineering: target the "internal champions" — the ones with time to participate, who represent the general user (not the executive suite), and who have a wide network.

## The three questions Kallow poses

1. **Which community should you target?** Be specific — the consultant's line: when most founders describe their target audience, they "point to everybody in the Starbucks." Strava's Mark Gainey pointed to the four MAMILs at the next table.
2. **Who within that community should you approach first?** The dandelions, not the celebrities.
3. **How do you convince them?** Lower adoption cost ruthlessly. ChatGPT vs. the OpenAI Playground. Granola recording silently vs. a bot announcing itself. Cursor's in-file edits vs. copy-pasting from a chat.

## Why this matters for `sahana-wiki` specifically

The whole project is itself a wide-bridge play eventually — if Sahana's wiki ever goes public (Stage 7 of the [[../../docs/stages/roadmap]]), the question of *which community*, *which dandelions*, and *what's the adoption cost* will determine whether it spreads. Worth keeping in view as a North Star, even though Stage 7 is far off.

More immediately: this is one of the strongest articulations in the wiki of *why* the agent-native era doesn't reduce to "build better tools." It directly counters the implicit assumption in many agent-native pieces that good architecture wins. It doesn't — sociology does.
