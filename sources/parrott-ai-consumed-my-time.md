# AI Was Supposed to Free My Time. It Consumed It.

## Central thesis

> *"AI feels different. It pulls us back because it feels good."*

AI doesn't liberate from work — it makes work irresistible. Where smartphones extend obligation into personal time, AI extends *desire* into personal time. Stopping feels irrational because the next prompt might be the one that solves the thing you've been stuck on for weeks.

## The Margot night

Parrott set out to spend "a few hours" configuring her AI assistant Margot (an OpenClaw-style Claw). Twelve hours later — at 1 a.m. — she was *"launching out of bed, rushing to desk, typing in all caps: 'OH MY GOD, MARGOT.'"* She'd rewritten essays, rebuilt her website, and added features to her apps. Her dog stood by the food bowl, an hour past dinner.

This is the article's emblem: the slot-machine pull of an unbounded-capability collaborator that never says "I'm tired."

## Mechanisms of the trap

### Task expansion

Parrott's role expanded to include *"building AI-powered editorial workflows"* and operations management alongside writing — work that would have been three jobs before. AI made unfamiliar tasks feel accessible, so she absorbed responsibilities she would have outsourced.

### Boundary blur

Unlike laptops or email, prompting *"feels closer to chatting than to formal labor."* Workers send "quick last prompts" before leaving the desk. The work crosses into personal time without any obligation cue.

### Cognitive overload

Multiple AI conversations in parallel feel productive in the moment but the context-switching is brutal. Parrott: her brain feels *"like a browser with 40 tabs open and not enough RAM to run any of them."*

## Psychological mechanisms

### Variable-ratio reinforcement (B.F. Skinner)

The most powerful behavioral conditioning pattern: unpredictable rewards after random attempts. The mechanism behind slot machines. Most AI responses are decent, some fail, occasional brilliant outputs sustain the addiction.

### "AI genie phenomenon" (M. Karen Shen, UBC)

Having access to *"an all-powerful assistant"* makes stopping feel irrational. *"Why close the laptop when the next prompt might solve the problem you've been stuck on for weeks?"*

### Epistemic rabbit holes

Each partial response opens new questions. *"The partial satisfaction is the engine: it's enough to keep going, never enough to stop."*

### FOBO — Fear of Becoming Obsolete

Ernst & Young 2025 survey: 54% of employees feel behind peers in AI adoption; 85% learn on personal time. *"The dopamine and the dread feed each other."*

## Practical responses

1. **Recognize the addiction mechanism.** Naming it (variable-ratio, AI genie) reframes overwork from dedication to compulsion.
2. **Use analog stopping points.** Bible study, board games, dog walks — activities AI workflows lack.
3. **Break isolation by talking about it.** Sharing the "I was up til 2" story normalizes struggle.
4. **Build organizational friction.** Batched notifications, protected focus windows, mandatory social connection.
5. **Reframe sufficiency.** *"You're doing enough."*
