# The Boring Businesses That Will Dominate the AI Era

## Opening scene

> *"At 2:17 a.m., your customer fires you."*

An agent has calculated that a $30,000 SaaS contract delivers only $12,000 of value, and switches without ceremony. The piece's central provocation: *"Agents don't need training like human users of software, and they don't have loyalty."* They make millisecond economic decisions with no switching cost.

## Central thesis

> *"What endures is infrastructure AI must flow through, but cannot replace."*

When the algorithms commoditize, *"the competitive edge shifts from having the best model to owning infrastructure."* The dull layer between algorithmic decisions and real-world consequences — data, compliance, workflow templates, domain processes — is what compounds with usage and becomes irreplaceable.

## Five archetypes

### 1. Knowledge compounders

Companies controlling proprietary, organized data that improves through real-world usage.

- **Medal.tv**: gaming community generates *"over 2 million gaming clips per day — more than 700 million per year,"* each carrying behavioral signal. Replicating it cold would cost hundreds of millions; Medal's users *"generate it organically in their quest for recognition."*
- **Mercor**: human-in-the-loop verification from domain experts (lawyers, doctors). High-stakes domains keep needing human judgment regardless of model capability.

### 2. Workflow commons

Platforms whose value is the accumulated operational templates other people built.

- **n8n**: hosts *"over 7,000 community-built workflow templates,"* each a production-tested blueprint. Valuation went from ~$300M to $2.5B between early 2025 and fall 2025 — *"accumulated workflows becoming the foundation for AI agent coordination."* An agent cannot reverse-engineer thousands of templates.

### 3. Reality's gatekeepers

Compliance and regulatory infrastructure that AI cannot legally bypass.

- **Stripe** (2.9% + 30¢), **Plaid**, **Deel**, **Column**: toll booths between code and consequence.
- The Synapse collapse left *"$85 million in customer funds stuck in limbo"* because no one could verify account ownership. Mercury survived by migrating to Column (a bank built for fintech).
- *"Trust, once earned, hardens into rebar, painful to rip out and expensive to rebuild."*

### 4. Marketplaces / trading floors for agents

Standardized protocols for agents to negotiate price, verify delivery, arbitrate disputes.

- **OpenRouter**: routes requests across models with different speeds, prices, reliability. *"Every routed request teaches the platform something: which models actually perform"* — leverage compounds with usage.
- Risk: this archetype depends entirely on model fragmentation. Convergence would collapse the routing value.

### 5. Vertical transformers

End-to-end replacement of an entire workflow in a regulated industry.

- **Harvey** in legal: McKinsey charges *"$2 million for three-month reviews"* with eight consultants; Harvey-class systems might deliver comparable output in *"48 hours for $20,000."* Defensibility = domain knowledge + compliance + liability management bundled together.

## The agent-as-customer reframe

The whole essay reframes "who is the customer." When the customer is an agent, every UX moat (good design, brand affinity, switching friction) evaporates. The remaining moats are operational: data you accumulate, compliance you've earned, workflows codified, regulatory access purchased.

## Why this matters for `sahana-wiki`

This is the macro counterpart to Sahana's *super-porous-ecosystem* framing. If the supply side of the agent era is atoms (prompts, tools, skills, MCPs, agents) physically composed by the user, then He's argument is about the demand side: where does economic gravity collect when those atoms are interchangeable? Answer: at the boundaries with reality — data, compliance, accumulated workflow, domain liability. The wiki itself is a knowledge compounder of one.
