# How I Escaped AI Autopilot

## Central thesis

> *"The central risk of AI-assisted work is not the machine thinking for you. It is the machine making it feel as if you already thought."*

When AI output is polished enough to *feel* reviewed, you stop actually reviewing it. The smoother the model gets, the less likely you are to catch what it gets wrong. The fix is friction — interventions that force you to do the hard part yourself.

## The duplicate-assignment incident

Four weeks after submitting a client deliverable, Parrott reread the brief and recognized everything: the phrasing, the statistics, the structure. She'd done this exact assignment before — and erased it from memory while racing through AI-delegated work. The labor never registered as labor.

> *"I had outsourced my mind."*

Not just labor. *Mind.*

## The mechanism

### Fluency = truth bias

A 1999 psychology study: people rated identical statements as more truthful when shown in easier-to-read fonts. AI compounds this: grammatically polished, confidently toned, cleanly formatted output creates an illusion of correctness.

> *"Your brain takes 'that was easy to process' and misfiles it as 'that must be correct.'"*

Each model improvement worsens the problem. Fewer obvious errors → remaining mistakes get harder to detect.

### Pilot autopilot, automation paradox

1990s cockpit studies: pilots followed incorrect automated recommendations *despite* contradictory instruments. A 2010 review codified the pattern:

> *"The more reliable an automated system becomes, the more likely humans are to let it pass unchecked."*

### "Reviewed" feeling vs. actual review

Parrott distinguishes the **warm sensation** of having reviewed work from the **hard cognitive labor** of actual review — checking claims, noticing assumptions, asking whether sentences would survive scrutiny months later.

### Cognitive forcing functions

Research on reducing AI overreliance: designs that force people to form an independent judgment *before* seeing the AI answer **cut mistaken acceptances roughly in half** — but users rated them poorly because friction is unpleasant. The tension between effort reduction and quality control is real and structural.

## Three interventions

### 1. Think before you look

Write your own rough position first — five bullets: initial thoughts, knowledge gaps, non-negotiables, intended reader value. Then consult AI. The five bullets become the comparison framework. Without them, your only check on the AI is "vibes."

### 2. Build temporal gaps

Separate generation from review by hours or a day. Reviewing N AI outputs in sequence guarantees lower scrutiny on output N+1. Move output between surfaces (chat → document, mobile → desktop) to break automatic pattern recognition. *"This resurrects advice writing teachers have offered for decades."*

### 3. Make yourself explain why

Before accepting any recommendation, write one sentence: *"Why is this right for this specific client/argument/reader?"* If the best you can do is "it sounds good," reject it. *"Written defenses cannot be bullshitted."*

## Memorable lines

- *"The central risk of AI-assisted work is not the machine thinking for you. It is the machine making it feel as if you already thought."*
- *"The more reliable AI gets, the less we check its work."*
- *"Your brain takes 'that was easy to process' and misfiles it as 'that must be correct.'"*
- *"A tool that says, 'Before I help you, please do the hard part yourself' feels like a speed bump. But speed bumps are the solution to autopilot."*

## Design-level fix

Tools should make content provenance visible, separate generation from approval, and treat human judgment as a workflow stage rather than a final ceremonial click. Parrott highlights Every's **Proof** editor as one example — it tracks which words originated from humans vs. machines.
